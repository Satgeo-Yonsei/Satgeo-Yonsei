Who We Are

This is the webpage of the Satellite Geosciences Research Group at the Department of Earth System Sciences, Yonsei University. The group is led by Hahn Chul Jung, and the research that we do focuses on radar remote sensing, land information system, natural disaster monitoring, and the study of large-schale hydrology. What we do in our lab is as follows : satellite data processing and develop algorithms for earth observation, modeling implementation using satellite-based input data, analysis & understanding of natural phenomena using satellite data and modeling.

🛰️ Mudflats - nature's true changemakers! These incredible ecosystems are submerged half the day, constantly shifting as tides ebb and flow. South Korea's mudflats span 2,443.3 km² (about 4x Seoul's size!), requiring innovative monitoring approaches beyond traditional drone and vessel surveys. Our research leverages microwave satellite imagery to observe mudflats consistently regardless of weather conditions. By analyzing pixel brightness, exposure timing, and capture dates across images, we can map topography and exposure frequencies of these dynamic environments. This data is crucial for developing smart adaptation strategies as we face global sea level rise challenges. radar remote sensing geospatial information tidal flat

Our Satellite Earth Science Lab is creating high-resolution models of the Korean Peninsula's water systems by harnessing this sophisticated Earth modeling platform. The applications are vast: predicting floods and droughts, improving water resource management, and providing data-driven insights for environmental decision-making. 🛰️ Twin satellites that measure Earth's water by detecting tiny gravity changes? That's the incredible GRACE mission in action! These satellites fly in formation, precisely measuring distance changes between them caused by Earth's gravitational variations. This research focuses on overcoming GRACE's biggest limitation - its coarse spatial resolution (about 330km). While perfect for large-scale hydrological research like monitoring climate change impacts on water resources, this resolution is too broad for local studies. We're developing statistical downscaling techniques that combine GRACE data with various numerical models to enhance spatial resolution. This work has exciting applications for drought/flood prediction, groundwater depletion monitoring, and sea level variation analysis. The GRACE mission revolutionized how we track water movement across our planet - now we're taking that revolutionary data to the next level! GRACE water resources

🛰️ Measuring the ocean from space - where precision meets complexity! My research focuses on developing specialized altimetry data processing methods for coastal regions using diverse altimeter modes. Satellite altimeters work by measuring radar pulse round-trip time between satellite and surface to determine height. While calculating sea surface height (the distance from sea surface to Earth's reference ellipsoid) sounds straightforward, coastal areas present unique challenges. In these transition zones, radar signals capture mixed land-sea returns, and the data is significantly affected by tides and atmospheric conditions, making traditional sea level measurements difficult to utilize. To overcome these limitations, cutting-edge technologies like SAR mode altimeters (utilizing the Doppler effect) and FF-SAR mode altimeters are being developed to produce high-resolution sea surface height data. I'm working with these various altimeter modes to create processing techniques specifically optimized for coastal environments where conventional methods fall short. altimeter remote sensing algorithm development

🛰️ Field-based ground monitoring just can't keep up with detecting subsidence across large areas - that's where satellite technology shines! My research applies Persistent Scatterer Interferometric SAR ( PSInSAR) to monitor ongoing ground subsidence at Busan New Port and its hinterland. Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is revolutionizing how we track ground movement - it works regardless of weather conditions or time of day, making it ideal for continuous monitoring. By analyzing multiple SAR images of the same location over time, we can create incredibly precise time-series measurements of ground subsidence. This approach allows us to detect subtle changes that would be impossible to observe through traditional field methods, providing critical data for infrastructure safety and urban planning. The time-series analysis reveals patterns and progression rates that help predict and mitigate potential hazards. PSInSAR Interferometry SAR natural hazard
